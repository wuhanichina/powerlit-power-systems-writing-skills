# Section Quality Review

Use this reference when reviewing full manuscripts or any title/keywords, abstract, introduction, case-analysis, or conclusion section. It complements technical review by checking whether each manuscript component does its job.

## Title and Keywords

Check whether the title is:

- scientific, concise, and directly tied to the real technical object;
- clear about the power-system object, method or mechanism, and application/operating condition when needed;
- innovation-bearing without exaggerating evidence;
- not a pure acronym, slogan, venue buzzword list, or math-first title when the contribution is physical or engineering.

If no title is supplied, ask for one or propose a bounded title during revision planning. If the title is broader than the evidence, mark it as a claim-boundary issue.

Keywords should be precise and no more than five unless the venue requires otherwise. They should cover the system object, method object, application domain, and key mechanism or metric without duplication.

## Abstract

Review whether the abstract concisely covers:

- background or operating context;
- purpose or unresolved problem;
- method or technical object;
- result or evidence object;
- significance, practical value, and boundary when needed.

High-risk patterns:

- slow broad background before the technical subject appears;
- no result or evidence object;
- method described as a task list;
- innovation or practical value asserted without support;
- claims not later supported by case analysis or derivation.

## Introduction

The introduction should:

- move from broad background to the specific technical conflict quickly;
- explain current problems and challenges, not only topic importance;
- identify the technical essence of the problem: physical coupling, constraint, uncertainty, information limitation, computation, stability, security, observability, or evidence gap;
- condense a key scientific or engineering problem;
- synthesize recent high-level literature, preferably EI-indexed or above and mainly from the last five years when available;
- group literature by method family and limitation rather than listing papers one by one;
- state the paper's basic idea and relative advantage;
- transition naturally from background to research state, unresolved issue, proposed idea, and evidence boundary.

If the manuscript claims a high-level venue but relies mainly on outdated, low-level, or unrelated references for the active gap, mark this as a major introduction/reference risk. If the reviewer cannot verify the literature from supplied or retrieved sources, label it as external-check-needed rather than inventing missing literature.

## Case Analysis

Check whether the case analysis is designed for the innovation point:

- it states the theoretical or engineering problem being tested;
- data source, test system, scenario, and operating condition are complete and justified;
- baselines are present and fair for comparison claims;
- metrics include units and clear direction;
- comparison with existing methods is sufficient for claimed relative advantage;
- sensitivity, ablation, or boundary tests exist when parameters, modules, or assumptions matter;
- case scale and analysis depth match the target venue and claim class;
- every figure and table is interpreted, not merely displayed;
- conclusions drawn from cases are logically supported.

Major risk appears when the case study only proves the code runs, not that the proposed innovation works.

## Conclusion

Check whether the conclusion:

- briefly summarizes main content and innovation points;
- states findings supported by cases, derivations, or cited evidence;
- avoids both exaggeration and unnecessary self-weakening;
- stays concise and does not introduce new literature, new numbers, or new contributions;
- gives reasonable future work only when it follows from a real boundary.

Overbroad deployment, robustness, generality, or scalability claims should be downgraded unless directly supported.
